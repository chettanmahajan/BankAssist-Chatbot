TRADE FINANCE — LC, BG, EXPORT CREDIT, FOREX FOR BUSINESS

OVERVIEW
Trade finance is a banking specialty that supports international and domestic trade — facilitating payments, mitigating counterparty risk, providing working capital, and managing forex exposure. Indian banks act as Authorised Dealers (AD Category-I) under FEMA, 1999, to handle foreign exchange and trade finance products. Regulated by RBI, FEDAI (Foreign Exchange Dealers Association of India), DGFT, and Customs. Rates and figures are indicative; rates may change, verify with bank.

LETTER OF CREDIT (LC)

What it is: A guarantee issued by the buyer's bank (Issuing Bank) to the seller (Beneficiary), promising payment upon submission of specified documents conforming to LC terms. LC mitigates seller's risk of non-payment and buyer's risk of non-shipment.

Parties:
- Applicant: Buyer (importer)
- Beneficiary: Seller (exporter)
- Issuing Bank: Buyer's bank
- Advising Bank: Seller's local bank (advises the LC to seller)
- Confirming Bank (optional): Adds its own guarantee to LC
- Negotiating Bank: Pays seller against documents
- Reimbursing Bank: Pays the negotiating bank
- Nominated Bank: Authorised to pay / accept / negotiate

Types of LC:

1. Sight LC: Payment on presentation of compliant documents (immediate payment)

2. Usance / Time / Term LC: Payment after specified period (e.g., 30/60/90/180 days from sight or shipment date) — gives buyer credit period

3. Revocable LC: Can be modified/cancelled without beneficiary consent (rarely used; high risk)

4. Irrevocable LC: Cannot be changed without all parties' consent (standard, default per UCP 600)

5. Confirmed LC: A second bank confirms (adds its guarantee) — usually used in high country-risk scenarios

6. Standby LC (SBLC): Used as a guarantee (like a bank guarantee); paid only if applicant defaults

7. Transferable LC: Beneficiary can transfer rights to a third party (used in intermediary trade)

8. Back-to-back LC: Beneficiary uses incoming LC as security for issuing another LC to its supplier

9. Red Clause LC: Allows beneficiary to draw advance against the LC before shipment

10. Green Clause LC: Like Red Clause + warehousing facility

11. Revolving LC: Auto-renews up to specified amount and/or period

12. Negotiation / Acceptance / Sight LC: By type of payment

Documentation under LC:
- Commercial Invoice
- Bill of Lading (or Air Waybill, Truck Receipt)
- Packing List
- Certificate of Origin
- Insurance Policy / Certificate
- Inspection Certificate
- Beneficiary's Certificate
- Phytosanitary / Health Certificate (for agri / pharma)
- Customs declaration

UCP 600 (Uniform Customs and Practice for Documentary Credits): International rules governing LC by ICC (International Chamber of Commerce). Standard reference document.

ISBP (International Standard Banking Practice): Document examination practices.

LC ISSUANCE PROCESS:
1. Buyer applies to bank with LC application form, commercial agreement
2. Bank assesses creditworthiness; may require margin (10%-100% based on credit profile)
3. LC drafted and issued via SWIFT MT700 message to beneficiary's bank
4. Beneficiary's bank advises LC to beneficiary
5. Beneficiary ships goods, prepares documents per LC
6. Documents presented to negotiating bank
7. Documents examined for LC compliance; payment made (sight) or accepted (usance)
8. Documents forwarded to issuing bank → buyer pays issuing bank → buyer receives documents → takes possession of goods

LC Charges:
- Issuance commission: 0.15%-0.5% per quarter on LC amount
- Amendment fee: ₹500-₹2,000 per amendment
- Negotiation/payment commission: 0.10%-0.20% on amount paid
- SWIFT charges: ₹500-₹2,500 per message
- Discrepancy fee: USD 100-200 per discrepancy
- Margin requirement (for buyer): 10%-100% of LC value, depending on credit profile
- Plus GST 18% on commissions and charges

LC ADVANTAGES
For Exporter:
- Payment assured if documents comply
- Counterparty risk shifted to bank
- Can negotiate pre-shipment / post-shipment credit

For Importer:
- Goods received per agreed quality / quantity (via inspection clauses)
- Payment only after shipment proof
- Credit period under usance LC

LC DISADVANTAGES
- Costs (commissions, charges)
- Time-consuming documentation
- Strict compliance needed; even small discrepancies can delay payment
- Bank examines documents, not actual goods

BANK GUARANTEE (BG)

What it is: A written commitment by the bank to pay a specified amount to a beneficiary in case the applicant (customer of bank) fails to meet certain contractual obligations.

Types:

1. Financial Bank Guarantee: Guarantees payment of a financial obligation (e.g., tender deposit, security deposit)

2. Performance Bank Guarantee: Guarantees performance of a contract (e.g., project completion, supply terms). If applicant fails to perform, beneficiary can invoke BG.

3. Advance Payment Guarantee: Protects buyer who has made advance payment to seller, in case seller fails to deliver

4. Bid Bond / Earnest Money Deposit BG: For tender participation

5. Retention Money BG: Allows contractor to receive retention money instead of bank holding it

6. Deferred Payment Guarantee (DPG): For import of capital goods on deferred payment terms

Common Uses:
- Tender bidding (bid bond)
- Performance security in construction / supply contracts
- Customs / excise duty deposit
- Statutory / regulatory body deposits (electricity, court, government)
- Import advance payments

BG Issuance Process:
1. Customer applies with bank with format / requirement of BG
2. Bank assesses credit; may require margin or collateral
3. BG issued in specified format on bank's letterhead
4. Beneficiary receives BG
5. BG remains valid until expiry; can be extended on customer's request
6. If applicant defaults, beneficiary invokes BG and bank pays

BG Charges:
- Commission: 0.50%-1.5% per annum on BG amount (depends on tenure and risk)
- Issuance fee: ₹500-₹2,000
- Amendment fee: ₹500-₹1,500
- Margin: 0%-100% (typically 10%-25% for established customers)
- Plus GST 18%

BG vs LC:
- LC: Bank pays in normal course if documents conform (positive payment)
- BG: Bank pays only if applicant defaults (contingent liability)

EXPORT CREDIT

Banks provide pre-shipment and post-shipment credit to exporters for working capital and bridging payment cycles.

PRE-SHIPMENT CREDIT (PACKING CREDIT)

Purpose: Working capital to purchase raw materials, manufacture, package, transport, and load goods for export.

Types:
- Packing Credit Loan (PCL) in INR
- Packing Credit in Foreign Currency (PCFC) — in USD, EUR, GBP, etc.

Features:
- Tenure: Up to 180 days from drawdown (extendable to 360 days with RBI approval for special cases)
- Interest: Concessional, regulated by RBI:
  - PCL (INR): MCLR / EBLR + risk premium, often 7%-9% with interest subvention
  - PCFC (foreign currency): LIBOR + premium / new benchmarks (SOFR) + spread, often 4%-6%
- Disbursed against firm export order / LC
- Repaid from export proceeds

Interest Equalisation Scheme (IES) / Interest Subvention:
- Government provides 3%-5% subvention on rupee export credit to MSME exporters
- Applicable to manufacturers and merchant exporters in identified sectors
- Reduces effective rate to as low as 4%-7%
- Check current applicability and rates

POST-SHIPMENT CREDIT

Purpose: Bridge credit after goods are shipped until export proceeds are realised.

Types:
- Negotiation of export bills (with bank discounting documents and paying upfront, recovering from buyer later)
- Foreign Currency Bills Purchased (FBP)
- Foreign Currency Bills Discounted (FBD)
- Advance against export bills sent on collection basis
- Advance against duty drawback / GST refund

Features:
- Tenure: Up to 180 days from shipment date (extendable for usance bills)
- Interest: Similar concessional rates as pre-shipment
- Repaid on receipt of export proceeds

EXPORT-IMPORT BANK OF INDIA (EXIM BANK)
Established 1982. Specialised bank for international trade:
- Buyer's credit to importers in developing countries to buy Indian goods
- Lines of credit to foreign governments / banks
- Export credit, project finance for exports
- Information and advisory

ECGC — EXPORT CREDIT GUARANTEE CORPORATION
Insures exporters against risks of buyer default, country risk, foreign exchange:
- Premium-based policies
- Up to 90% cover for confirmed export bills
- Mandatory in some export credit cases
- Provides ratings for foreign buyers

IMPORT FINANCE

Types:
1. Letter of Credit (Sight or Usance) — covered above

2. Buyer's Credit (Foreign): Foreign bank pays Indian exporter on behalf of Indian importer at lower foreign currency rate; Indian importer repays foreign bank later. Arranged via Indian bank acting as arranger.

3. Supplier's Credit: Foreign supplier extends credit to Indian importer directly; usually backed by LC or buyer's credit.

4. Term Loan for Capital Goods Import: Indian bank funds the import of machinery, paying foreign supplier and creating a term loan for importer.

5. Acceptance Credit Facility (ACU - Asian Clearing Union dealings).

FOREIGN EXCHANGE FOR BUSINESS

Forward Contracts:
- Lock in an exchange rate today for a future date
- Hedges against currency volatility
- Used by importers (lock against rupee depreciation) and exporters (lock against rupee appreciation)
- Tenure typically 30-365 days
- Margin requirement varies (5%-15%)

Options Contracts:
- Right (not obligation) to buy/sell foreign currency at strike rate
- Premium paid upfront
- Used for more flexible hedging
- Available for limited currency pairs

Spot Transactions:
- Immediate conversion at prevailing rate
- For day-to-day import/export remittances
- TT Buying / Selling rates apply

Cross-currency dealings:
- Direct conversion (e.g., EUR to GBP without going via USD or INR)
- Reduces conversion costs

EEFC ACCOUNT — EXCHANGE EARNERS' FOREIGN CURRENCY
- Resident exporters maintain foreign currency earnings without conversion to INR
- Up to 100% of export proceeds can be retained
- Useful when re-investing in imports or foreign payments
- No interest paid on EEFC
- Funds can be used for permitted purposes per FEMA

FCY-INR HEDGE STRATEGIES
1. Natural hedge: Match foreign currency inflows with outflows
2. Forward booking: Standard hedging tool
3. Options / structured products: For volatile markets
4. EEFC: Avoid conversion entirely
5. Bill of Exchange / Documentary Collection: Risk-reduction in trade

DOCUMENTARY COLLECTION (D/A and D/P)
Alternative to LC:
- D/P (Documents against Payment): Exporter ships goods, sends documents via bank; importer pays bank to get documents
- D/A (Documents against Acceptance): Importer accepts a Bill of Exchange (promise to pay at future date); receives documents; pays on maturity

Lower cost than LC but less secure (no bank guarantee of payment).

INDIAN TRADE PORTAL AND DIGITAL TOOLS
- DGFT IceGate (icegate.gov.in): Customs and trade portal
- TradeStat (commerce.gov.in): Trade statistics
- DGFT Portal (dgft.gov.in): Foreign trade policy, IEC, licences
- e-BRC (Bank Realisation Certificate) on DGFT portal for export proceeds proof
- Bharat Trade Portal — emerging digital initiative

IEC — IMPORTER EXPORTER CODE
- Mandatory 10-digit code from DGFT for any import/export
- Free, online application via dgft.gov.in
- PAN required
- One IEC per PAN
- Activated via PAN; no separate IEC application needed since 2022 (auto-linked)
- Free for life

ADVANCE AUTHORISATION / EPCG / DUTY DRAWBACK
DGFT schemes to support exporters with duty-free imports of inputs:
- Advance Authorisation: Import inputs duty-free for export production
- EPCG (Export Promotion Capital Goods): Import capital goods at concessional / zero duty against export obligation
- Duty Drawback: Refund of customs duty paid on inputs after export
- MEIS / SEIS (now subsumed under RoDTEP — Remission of Duties and Taxes on Exported Products): Export incentives

TRADE FINANCE CHARGES — SUMMARY (INDICATIVE)
- LC issuance: 0.15%-0.5% per quarter
- BG issuance: 0.5%-1.5% per annum
- LC/BG amendment: ₹500-₹2,500
- Negotiation: 0.10%-0.20%
- Foreign currency cheque collection: USD 5-50 + bank fees
- Outward remittance for imports: ₹500-₹2,000 per transaction + forex markup
- Forward booking: 0.05%-0.10% commission + forex spread
- SWIFT message: ₹250-₹2,500 per message
- Plus GST 18% on most service fees

EXPORT PROCEEDS REALISATION
Per FEMA / RBI guidelines:
- Exporter must realise and repatriate export proceeds within 9 months from date of export (some sectors 6 months) — extended periodically
- Non-realisation is FEMA contravention
- Bank submits BRC (Bank Realisation Certificate) on receipt
- Caution List: Repeated non-realisation can lead to inclusion in RBI Caution List restricting future exports

GST FOR EXPORTERS
- Exports are zero-rated under GST
- Two options:
  - Export with LUT (Letter of Undertaking) — no IGST payment; refund of accumulated ITC
  - Export with IGST payment — refund of IGST after shipment
- LUT obtained annually from GST portal

KEY REGULATORY AUTHORITIES
- RBI: Foreign exchange, banking aspect
- DGFT (Directorate General of Foreign Trade): Foreign trade policy, licences, IEC, schemes
- Customs / CBIC: Customs duties, clearance
- FEDAI (Foreign Exchange Dealers Association of India): Exchange rates, market practices
- FIEO (Federation of Indian Export Organisations): Trade body representing exporters
- EXIM Bank, ECGC

DOMESTIC LC AND BG
LCs and BGs are also used for domestic trade between Indian buyers and sellers:
- Inland LC: For domestic transactions, especially when buyer-seller are in different states or new relationship
- Domestic BG: Common in construction, government tenders, supplier contracts

KEY TAKEAWAYS

For Importers:
1. Get IEC registered
2. Choose between LC, BG, advance payment, open account based on supplier relationship and country risk
3. Hedge foreign currency exposure via forward contracts
4. Manage payment terms in trade agreements
5. Comply with FEMA, customs, GST

For Exporters:
1. Negotiate LC at sight for new buyers; usance for established
2. Use pre-shipment and post-shipment credit for working capital
3. ECGC insurance for buyer / country risk
4. Hedge export receivables
5. Realise proceeds within 9 months
6. Claim duty drawback / RoDTEP / GST refunds
7. Use EEFC for partial currency retention

For Both:
1. Build relationship with trade-friendly bank
2. Use technology — DGFT portal, IceGate, bank's trade portals
3. Stay updated on FTP (Foreign Trade Policy) and RBI circulars
4. Maintain proper documentation for audit and statutory needs
5. Consider Forward Contracts and Options for risk management

Rates and rules may change, verify with bank / RBI / DGFT for current trade finance products, schemes, and procedures.
