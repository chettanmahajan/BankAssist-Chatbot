EDUCATION LOAN — COMPREHENSIVE GUIDE

OVERVIEW
An education loan is a financial product that funds higher education expenses of Indian students for studies in India or abroad. It covers tuition fees, hostel and accommodation, library fees, examination and laboratory fees, books and equipment (including a laptop), travel expenses for overseas study, and a margin for project work, study tours, and other reasonable expenditure. The Indian Banks' Association (IBA) has framed a Model Education Loan Scheme that public sector banks broadly follow, with some variations. Private banks like HDFC Credila, Axis Bank, ICICI Bank, and NBFCs like Avanse, InCred, and Auxilo also offer education loans. Rates and terms below are indicative; rates may change, verify with bank.

ELIGIBILITY
Student eligibility:
- Indian national, having secured admission to a recognised course through merit-based selection or entrance test
- Age 18 to 35 years (more flexible for postgraduate and professional courses)
- For undergraduate studies, the student must have completed Class 12 or equivalent
- For postgraduate studies, must have completed UG with reasonable scores
- For executive programs, work experience may be required

Co-applicant: Parent or guardian must be a co-applicant. For loans above ₹4 lakh, a co-applicant is mandatory under the IBA scheme. The co-applicant should have a stable income and acceptable credit history.

LOAN AMOUNT
The IBA Model Scheme sets the following indicative limits:
- Studies in India: Up to ₹10 lakh
- Studies abroad: Up to ₹20 lakh
However, many banks lend up to ₹40 lakh for India and ₹75 lakh or more for abroad based on the institution and course. Premier institutions (IITs, IIMs, top medical colleges, Ivy League and top global universities) often qualify for higher loans without collateral. NBFCs like HDFC Credila offer loans up to ₹2 crore for studies abroad at premium universities.

COLLATERAL REQUIREMENTS
- Up to ₹4 lakh: No collateral; no margin; only co-borrower (parent) required.
- ₹4 lakh to ₹7.5 lakh: No collateral generally, but a third-party guarantee is required (a person of means who guarantees repayment). 5% margin for India studies.
- Above ₹7.5 lakh: Tangible collateral is required — typically immovable property (residential or commercial), fixed deposits, government securities, LIC policies with surrender value, NSC, KVP, gold (in some banks). 15% margin for studies abroad.
The collateral value should generally be 1 to 1.25 times the loan amount.

INSTITUTION CATEGORIES
Most banks classify institutions into categories:
- AA / Premier: IITs, IIMs, AIIMS, NITs, top global universities (Harvard, Stanford, MIT, Oxford, Cambridge etc.) — best rates, larger amounts, often no/low collateral
- A / Strong: Tier-1 Indian universities, top private institutions, well-ranked global universities
- B / Standard: Other recognised universities and colleges
Premier-category students get the best interest rates, fastest approval, and higher unsecured limits.

INTEREST RATES
Education loan interest rates in India typically range from 8.30% per annum to 14.5% per annum:
- Public sector banks (SBI, BoB, PNB): 8.30%-11.5% for premium institutions; 9.5%-12.5% for standard
- Private banks: 10%-13.5%
- NBFCs (Credila, Avanse, InCred, Auxilo): 10.5%-14.5%
Female students typically get a 0.5% concession. Loans for studies in premier institutions get the lowest rates. Floating rates linked to RLLR/EBLR are common at public sector banks; private banks and NBFCs often offer fixed rates. Simple interest is charged during the course period and moratorium; once EMI starts, interest is compounded monthly. Rates may change, verify with bank.

MORATORIUM PERIOD
A defining feature of education loans is the moratorium (repayment holiday): the borrower does not need to start full EMI repayment immediately. Moratorium = course duration + 6 to 12 months grace period after course completion (to allow the student time to secure a job). For a 2-year MBA, moratorium = 2 years + 1 year = 3 years total. Some banks ask borrowers to pay simple interest during the moratorium voluntarily (which gives a 1% interest concession on outstanding); others capitalise the interest into the principal at the start of EMI.

REPAYMENT TENURE
After the moratorium ends, repayment is in EMIs over 5 to 15 years. Total tenure (moratorium + repayment) can be up to 15-20 years. Prepayment of education loans typically has no charges — borrowers are encouraged to repay early.

DOCUMENTS REQUIRED
Student:
- Admission letter from recognised institution
- Course fee structure and breakup
- Mark sheets and certificates of all previous qualifying examinations
- Entrance exam scorecard (GRE, GMAT, JEE, NEET, CAT etc.)
- Passport and visa (for abroad)
- Photographs and KYC

Co-applicant (parent/guardian):
- KYC documents: PAN, Aadhaar, address proof
- Income proof: Last 2 years' ITR, last 3 months' salary slips, last 6 months' bank statements
- For self-employed: ITR, business proof
- Statement of assets and liabilities

Collateral documents (for loans needing security):
- Title deed of the property being pledged
- Property valuation certificate
- Encumbrance certificate
- Latest property tax receipts
- For FD/NSC/LIC: original certificates / policy documents

TAX BENEFIT — SECTION 80E
Section 80E of the Income Tax Act allows a deduction on the interest paid on an education loan for higher studies (after Class 12) for the borrower, spouse, or children. Key points:
- Only the interest component is deductible; principal is NOT eligible
- No upper limit on the deduction amount
- Available for a maximum of 8 consecutive assessment years starting from the year EMI begins, or until the interest is fully paid, whichever is earlier
- Loan must be taken from a recognised financial institution or notified charitable institution
- Applies to studies in India and abroad
This makes long-tenure loans tax-efficient — pay slower, save more tax.

VIDYA LAKSHMI PORTAL
The Government of India operates the Vidya Lakshmi Portal at vidyalakshmi.co.in — a single-window online platform where students can apply for education loans across 40+ banks. The portal also tracks application status and integrates with the Central Sector Interest Subsidy Scheme (CSIS). Highly recommended for first-time applicants because it simplifies comparison across banks.

CENTRAL SECTOR INTEREST SUBSIDY SCHEME (CSIS)
Under CSIS, the Government of India bears 100% of the interest during the moratorium period for economically weaker students. Eligibility:
- Annual family income up to ₹4.5 lakh
- Loan up to ₹7.5 lakh
- Studies in India in technical and professional courses approved by NAAC / NBA / AICTE
After the moratorium, the borrower starts paying EMI which includes interest. This is a powerful benefit — saves lakhs over the moratorium period.

DR. AMBEDKAR SCHEMES / PADHO PARDESH
Dr. Ambedkar Central Sector Scheme of Interest Subsidy for OBC and EBC students who study abroad at Master's, M.Phil, and PhD levels. Padho Pardesh (now subsumed under similar schemes) supported minority community students. Both reimburse interest during the moratorium for eligible candidates. Check current scheme status on the Ministry of Social Justice and Empowerment website.

SKILL LOAN SCHEME
A separate scheme for vocational courses with shorter durations (2 months to 3 years). Loan amount ₹5,000 to ₹1.5 lakh. No collateral required. Repayment 3 to 7 years. Aimed at promoting skill development among youth.

TOP TIPS FOR EDUCATION LOAN APPLICANTS
1. Start the application process at least 3 months before the course start date.
2. Apply through Vidya Lakshmi for transparent comparison.
3. Public sector banks generally offer the lowest rates; NBFCs offer faster processing.
4. If pledging collateral, get the valuation done from a bank-approved valuer.
5. Read the sanction letter carefully — note moratorium terms, interest capitalisation, prepayment terms.
6. Get a Forex card and overseas student bank account opened in advance for abroad studies.
7. Maintain a healthy CIBIL score of the co-applicant; this hugely affects approval and rate.
8. Pay simple interest during moratorium voluntarily if cash flow allows — it earns a rate concession.
9. After job placement, prepay aggressively to reduce total interest outflow.

RBI GUIDELINES AND CUSTOMER PROTECTION
Education loans fall under priority sector lending. Banks must offer them on transparent and uniform terms within their scheme. Banks cannot deny a loan to a deserving student citing lack of collateral if the loan amount is up to ₹7.5 lakh. Grievances can be raised with the bank, then escalated to the RBI Banking Ombudsman via cms.rbi.org.in. Rates may change, verify with bank for current offers.
